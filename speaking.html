01 / Pausing

When to pause — and why.

A pause is not dead air. It is a tool. The best speakers pause on purpose — to let ideas land, to signal importance, and to give the audience time to think.

Short — 1 sec
Let a single idea land. Before listing items. After a key name or number.
Medium — 2 sec
Shift between topics. Before revealing something important. Look at your audience.
Long — 3 sec
After a big statement. After a punch line. After "this changes everything."
Breath — inhale
New section start. After a long list. Reset your voice and posture.
Rule 01

Pause before the most important word

Not after — before. The pause before creates anticipation. The pause after creates reflection. Use both for maximum impact.

"The one thing that changed everything was… [2 sec] …accessibility."
Rule 02

Never fill silence with filler words

"Um," "uh," "like," "you know" destroy your authority instantly. Train yourself to replace every filler with silence. Silence sounds confident. Fillers sound uncertain.

Rule 03

The 3-second pause after a big claim

When you make a strong statement — a surprising statistic, a bold conclusion — pause for 3 full seconds. It feels long to you and perfect to the audience.

"AI will automate tasks we thought only humans could do." [3 sec] Then continue.
Rule 04

Pause and make eye contact simultaneously

During a long pause, look at one person in the audience for 2 seconds. Then move to another. This makes every individual feel personally addressed.

03 / Persuasion

How to convince.

Convincing someone is not about being louder. It is about understanding what they need to hear — and delivering it in a way that feels like their own conclusion.

Technique 01

Start with agreement, not argument

Before making your case, find something genuine to agree with. This disarms defensiveness and signals you have listened. People are far more open to changing their mind when they feel heard first.

"You're absolutely right that cost is critical. And that's exactly why I want to show you why this investment pays back in 13 months."
Technique 02

Use "because" — always give a reason

People are significantly more likely to comply with a request when a reason is given — even a simple one. Never make a recommendation without the word "because" followed by a clear rationale.

"I recommend Feature B first because its payback period is 40% shorter, which directly reduces our Q3 cash risk."
Technique 03

The "so that" bridge — connect feature to outcome

Never pitch a feature — pitch the outcome. Everything you propose must end in a business consequence. Use the bridge: "[feature] so that [business outcome]."

"We integrate PLM with SAP in real time so that change cycles drop from 5 days to 1 — 80% faster time to market."
Technique 04

The Rule of Three — group your arguments

Present exactly three reasons for your recommendation. Not one — too thin. Not five — they'll forget. Three is the cognitive sweet spot. Name them before you explain them.

"There are three reasons to adopt agentic AI now: cost reduction, cycle time improvement, and competitive differentiation."
Technique 05

Acknowledge the objection before they raise it

The most powerful persuasion move is to name the other person's objection before they do. It signals you have thought about it, removes their ammunition, and lets you answer it on your terms.

"I know what you're thinking — this sounds expensive. Let me show you the three-year ROI first."
Technique 06

The "what would it take?" close

When someone is reluctant, don't push harder — ask what evidence would change their mind. This shifts from disagreement to requirements, which is solvable.

"What information or evidence would help you feel more confident about this direction?"
04 / Tough Situations

Handling tough questions.

Every speaker faces questions they didn't expect. The difference is knowing how to handle not knowing.

I don't know
You don't know the answer. What do you say?
+
Never bluff. Being caught bluffing is far more damaging than admitting you don't know.

1
Acknowledge directly: "That's a great question — I want to give you an accurate answer rather than a guessed one."
2
Show your reasoning: "What I do know is [related context], which suggests the answer is likely in the direction of [reasoned guess]."
3
Commit to follow up: "I'll confirm the exact figure and get back to you by end of today." Then actually do it.
Admitting you don't know builds more credibility than a confident wrong answer.
Hostile
Someone challenges you aggressively or tries to embarrass you in front of others.
+
The audience is watching how you react more than what you say. Stay calm — your composure is your strongest response.

1
Pause 2 seconds before responding. Maintaining eye contact while pausing signals confidence, not fear.
2
Separate tone from content. Respond to the question, not the aggression: "That's an important challenge — let me address the substance of it."
3
If they get louder, get quieter. Lowering your voice forces them and the audience to lean in, shifting power to you.
Trap
You get a "gotcha" question designed to make you look bad.
+
"Gotcha" questions often contain a false premise or force a binary answer on a complex issue. Don't accept the framing.

1
Name the false premise: "I'd push back slightly on the framing — it assumes X, but in practice Y is also true."
2
Reframe before answering: "The more useful question is [reframe], and the answer to that is…"
3
Stay constructive — don't make the questioner an enemy. Reframe as "understandable confusion."
Blank
Your mind goes completely blank mid-sentence.
+
This happens to everyone, including the most experienced speakers. How you handle it determines whether the audience notices.

1
Pause and breathe. The audience interprets a pause as thoughtfulness, not failure.
2
Use a bridging phrase: "Let me come back to the key point here…" or "What's most important from this section is…"
3
Return to structure. Every presentation has a structure — return to the big picture: "So we've covered X, and next I want to talk about Y."
Prevention: Never memorize word-for-word. Memorize your three main points — if you lose the words, you still have the points.
05 / Voice

Master your voice.

Your voice is an instrument. Most speakers use one setting. Variation is what creates impact.

Speed

Vary deliberately. Fast for excitement. Slow for gravity and importance.

✓ Slow down on power phrases
✗ Never speak at one constant speed

Volume

Lower your voice to make people lean in. Raise it to signal energy.

✓ Go quieter on climax moments
✗ Never get louder to win an argument

Pitch

Drop your pitch at the end of statements. Rising pitch sounds like questions.

✓ End statements with downward pitch
✗ Never end a statement going up

Emphasis

Stress one key word per sentence — not several. Over-emphasis means nothing is emphasized.

✓ One stressed word per sentence
✗ Never stress 3+ words per sentence
06 / Eye Contact

Command the room.

Eye contact is the single most powerful tool for connecting with an audience. Most speakers get it wrong.

👁️

One person at a time — complete a thought

Give one person eye contact for the full length of a thought. One thought = one person. Then move on.

📐

Cover the triangle

In a group, make a triangle — left, right, center. Return to each point. Nobody feels ignored.

⏸️

Eye contact during pauses is power

During LONG pauses, slowly look around the room without speaking. Uncomfortable for you. Magnetic for the audience.

💻

Video calls — camera, not the screen

Looking at their face on screen makes your camera see you looking down. Look directly at the camera lens. Put a sticky dot next to it as a reminder.

"Speak less. Mean more. Pause deliberately. Own every room you walk into."
