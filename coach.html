01 / Tell Me About Yourself

Your Director Script

Reveal each part in sequence — speak it out loud every time. Target: 2–3 minutes total. Parts 1, 2, 4, 5 are essential. Part 3 only if asked for more detail.

①
Opening Line
~20 sec
"I am a technology leader with 20+ years of experience driving digital transformation for global enterprises. My career spans three distinct phases, each one building deliberately on the last."
The phrase "three distinct phases, each building deliberately" signals strategic self-awareness — a Director trait. Avoid saying "I have been working for 20 years" — passive, not powerful.
②
Core Identity
~20 sec
"I am a Certified Product Owner and Capability Leader specialising in Strategic Portfolio Management, PLM, distributed systems integration, and multi-tool workflow architecture. I connect technical architecture to real business outcomes."
End with "I connect technical architecture to real business outcomes" — this is your value proposition in one sentence. Directors don't just build things, they create outcomes.
③
20-Year Journey (use only if asked for more detail)
~50 sec optional
2006–09 · Medisoft: Built core database architecture and PLM modules
2009–11 · HP: Led distributed workflow enhancements, HP OpenView enterprise transitions
2011–18 · Schneider — Technical Lead: Architected multi-tool workflows and cross-system integrations
2018–20 · Schneider — Country Manager: Led 14-member team, global digital migration
2020–22 · Schneider — Portfolio Expert: PLM roadmaps and cross-system data governance
2022–Now · Schneider — Certified PO + Capability Leader: Multi-year PLM roadmaps and GCC transformation
Each role was a deliberate step — say that. "Each role prepared me for the next." Do not recite dates unless they ask for the timeline.
④
Bridge → GCC Transformation Story
~30 sec
"Most recently, I was part of a C-Level strategic initiative — Raghu's vision to build a Global Capability Center in India. I played three progressive roles: Country Manager building the India team, Program Manager leading complex project migration from France, and Integration Leader bridging two systems across two countries. My VP Michel Francis was closely aligned throughout."
Say "Raghu" and "Michel Francis" clearly — named leadership proves you operated at that level. The three roles in one sentence shows progression without being long.
⑤
Closing — What Drives You
~20 sec
"What excites me most is connecting technical architecture to business outcomes — building roadmaps that deliver real ROI, not just working software. I hold a Master's in AI, completed while working full-time, because I believe leaders must keep learning."
End here. Stop talking. The interviewer will respond. Silence after a strong close is powerful — do not fill it with more words.
Key numbers woven in
$2.4B Portfolio 16 WebOnyx Releases GCC Built from Zero OCP Migration Lead Certified PO · 4 Years MTech AI (while working) Verizon, AT&T, AccuWeather
02 / Three Challenges

Your GCC Story

Three progressive challenges. Each uses the CARL framework: Context → Actions → Result → Learning.

🏗️ Challenge 1 · Country Manager
Building the GCC Support Team in India · Strategic — C-Level Vision by Raghu
C
Context — Set the scene
+
"The C-Level vision from Raghu was clear — build a GCC in India to replicate and eventually replace the France operations. Nothing existed in India. We had to build from zero — team, capability, process, and culture."
A
Actions — What YOU specifically did
+
  • Recruited and onboarded India support team from scratch
  • Designed the operating model — how India would mirror France operations
  • Coached team functionally and technically — I was the knowledge bridge between France expertise and India capability
  • Worked closely with VP Michel Francis to align on expectations and milestones
  • Ensured India team was not just a back-office — but a capable, empowered GCC
R
Result — Concrete outcome (Before → After)
+
"Successfully established a functioning GCC in India — team was built, trained, and operational. This became the foundation for challenges 2 and 3."
L
Learning — One sentence, shows self-awareness
+
"Building a GCC is not about hiring people — it's about transferring institutional knowledge and instilling ownership culture in a new geography."
🚀 Challenge 2 · Program Manager + Technical Coach
Moving Complex Project France → India · Technical + People Leadership
C
Context
+
"A highly complex, legacy-heavy project needed migration from France to India. The France team had years of deep knowledge. The India team was new to this system. The risk of knowledge loss was very high."
A
Actions
+
  • I was the longest-serving person on the system — personally led all knowledge transfer sessions
  • Created system complexity maps, architecture documentation, decision logs
  • Introduced a buddy model — France engineers paired with India counterparts
  • Coached India team both functionally (product understanding) and technically (system architecture)
  • Coached 3 senior POs — including a Director-level and Account Head
  • Managed stakeholder confidence with VP Michel Francis through structured regular updates
R
Result
+
"India team moved from fully dependent → independently capable. Project migrated successfully without major delivery failure. Knowledge was distributed, documented, and no longer a single-person risk."
L
Learning
+
"Migration is a people problem disguised as a technical problem. The technology can be documented — but confidence, capability, and culture must be built deliberately."
🔗 Challenge 3 · Integration Program Manager
Integrating Two Systems India + France · Cross-border Technical Integration
C
Context
+
"Even after migration, both teams needed to work together — India had the new system, France had the stable legacy system. Different maturity levels, time zones, and ways of working. No one was clearly owning the integration."
A
Actions
+
  • Mapped full integration architecture — data flows, APIs, dependency points
  • Created RACI matrix — assigned single DRI on each side
  • Established daily cross-border sync during overlapping time zones
  • Resolved team resistance by clarifying decision boundaries — people weren't refusing, they were confused
  • Built 3-horizon roadmap: stabilize now → optimize in 6 months → R&D migration in 12–24 months
R
Result
+
"Integration went from chaotic and unowned → structured and on-track. Both teams developed a collaborative working rhythm. Project moved from at-risk to controlled in portfolio reviews."
L
Learning
+
"Cross-border integrations break at the human layer before the technical layer. Timezone, trust, and clarity of ownership must be engineered as carefully as the systems themselves."
03 / Full Interview Script

Word-for-Word Answer

Read this 3× out loud, then speak without looking. Target: 3.5–4 minutes. Stop at 4 minutes no matter what.

1
Opening
30 sec
+
"I am a technology leader with 20+ years of experience driving digital transformation for global enterprises. I'm a Certified Product Owner and Capability Leader — my core expertise is Strategic Portfolio Management, PLM, distributed systems, and workflow architecture."
Pause here. Make eye contact. Let it land.
2
The GCC Vision
20 sec
+
"Most recently, I was part of a C-Level strategic initiative at Schneider Electric — a vision by our leadership, Raghu, to build a Global Capability Center in India, transitioning operations from France. My VP Michel Francis was closely aligned to this transformation."
Say "Raghu" and "Michel Francis" clearly — named leadership shows you operated at that level.
3
Challenge 1 — Build Team
45 sec
+
"I took on the Country Manager role. Nothing existed in India — zero team, zero process. I recruited and onboarded the India team from scratch, designed the operating model to mirror France operations, and personally served as the knowledge bridge. I coached the team both functionally and technically. The result — a fully functioning, empowered GCC that became the foundation for everything that followed."
Stress "from scratch" and "knowledge bridge" — these are your differentiators.
4
Challenge 2 — Migrate Project
45 sec
+
"Once the team was in place, the next challenge was migrating a highly complex, legacy-heavy project from France. The risk of knowledge loss was enormous. As the longest-serving person on the system, I personally led every knowledge transfer session. I created architecture maps, decision logs, and introduced a buddy model — France engineers paired with India counterparts. I also coached three senior POs, including a Director-level and Account Head. The India team went from fully dependent to independently capable — migration completed without major delivery failure."
"Longest-serving person" establishes unique authority. "Director-level" shows executive impact.
5
Challenge 3 — Integrate Systems
45 sec
+
"The final challenge — even after migration, both teams needed to work together. Different systems, different time zones, different maturity levels — and no one clearly owning the integration. I stepped in as Integration Program Manager. I mapped the full architecture, created a RACI matrix with a single DRI on each side, established daily cross-border syncs, and built a 3-horizon roadmap. Integration went from chaotic and unowned to structured and on-track. Both teams developed a genuine collaborative rhythm."
RACI, DRI, 3-horizon roadmap — these show senior program management vocabulary.
6
Closing Power Statement
20 sec
+
"At every stage — I was the person who held the technical knowledge, coached the people, and kept the strategic vision connected to ground-level execution. That is the kind of leader I am."
End here. Stop talking. Let the interviewer respond. Total target: 3.5–4 minutes.
04 / Follow-Up Q&A

Tough Follow-Ups

This is where interviews are won or lost. Think through your answer first — then reveal the coaching hint.

🏗️ Challenge 1 Follow-Ups
How did you handle resistance from the France team when building the GCC?
+
Answer angle: Show alignment through VP Michel Francis. Use structured communication — regular updates showed India team capability growing, not threatening France roles. Frame it as expansion, not replacement. The France team's expertise was being preserved and multiplied, not eliminated.
How long did it take to make the India GCC fully operational?
+
Answer angle: Be specific — define what "operational" meant in phases: team hired → first independent delivery → consistently performing. Show you had measurable milestones, not just activity. This demonstrates that you managed to outcomes, not tasks.
🚀 Challenge 2 Follow-Ups
What happened when a France engineer left during the migration?
+
Answer angle: Your buddy model and documentation was the answer. No single point of failure — the system map, decision logs, and paired counterparts meant knowledge was distributed. This is exactly why you built those risk mitigations before they were needed.
How did you coach senior POs including a Director-level person?
+
Answer angle: Show confidence — you had unique technical context they needed. It was collaborative, not hierarchical. You gave them system reality so their product decisions were grounded. Frame it as "enabling their decisions" not "teaching them".
🔗 Challenge 3 Follow-Ups
What was the biggest technical risk in the integration?
+
Answer angle: Data consistency across systems with different release cycles. API versioning between India's newer system and France's legacy. You addressed this in the architecture mapping — show you identified and documented these risks proactively, not reactively.
How did you manage time zone conflicts between India and France?
+
Answer angle: Daily sync in overlapping window (IST afternoon = France morning). Async documentation so decisions weren't blocked waiting for calls. RACI clarity meant each side could act independently within their boundary — reducing unnecessary cross-border dependencies.
⚡ Wild Card Questions
Why should we choose you over another candidate with similar experience?
+
Answer angle: Use your Power Statement. Then add: "Most people can manage projects OR coach people OR architect systems. I have done all three simultaneously, at scale, across geographies, aligned to a C-Level vision. That combination is rare."
Tell me about a time you failed.
+
Answer angle: Early in the GCC build, underestimated how long cultural alignment would take vs. technical onboarding. Show what you changed: introduced structured cultural onboarding alongside technical training. Failure + specific learning = growth mindset signal that senior leaders love.
"At every stage, I was the person who held the technical knowledge, coached the people, and kept the strategic vision connected to ground-level execution."
